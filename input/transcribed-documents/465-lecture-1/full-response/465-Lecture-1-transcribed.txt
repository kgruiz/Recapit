\noindent
\textbf{\large Content overview}

\vspace{0.2in}

\noindent
This course introduces the fundamental notions, techniques, and theorems of \textcolor{blue}{enumerative combinatorics} and \textcolor{blue}{graph theory}.

\vspace{0.1in}

\noindent
Level: undergraduate. Next level courses: \textcolor{blue}{Math 565}, \textcolor{blue}{Math 566}.

\vspace{0.1in}

\noindent
Recommended texts (neither is required):
\begin{itemize}
    \item M. Bóna, \textit{A walk through combinatorics}, 4\textsuperscript{th} ed., World Scientific, 2016-17.
    \item S. Shahriari, \textit{Invitation to Combinatorics}, Cambridge University Press, 2022.
\end{itemize}

\vspace{0.1in}

\noindent
We will roughly cover Part II (\textcolor{blue}{Enumerative Combinatorics}) and Part III (\textcolor{blue}{Graph Theory}) of Bóna's textbook, plus some other topics.

\vspace{0.1in}

\noindent
We will not follow either textbook too closely. The lectures and the books will often provide slightly different approaches.

This is a \textcolor{blue}{proof-based} course. Students are expected to understand
rigorous mathematical proofs, and perhaps even more importantly,
supply their own proofs when asked.

Some specific prerequisites:
\begin{itemize}
    \item proofs by induction,
    \item proofs by contradiction,
    \item basic set theory terminology (intersection, union, etc.),
    \item basic function terminology (one-to-one, onto, composition, etc.).
\end{itemize}

If you are not familiar with Mathematical Induction, read either
[Bóna, Chapter 2] or [Shahriari, Sections 1.1–1.2].

Count everything \textcolor{blue}{exactly} once. \\ \hline

\section*{}
\noindent Unordered set partitions

\noindent \textbf{Problem}

\noindent In how many ways can a 4-element set be partitioned into nonempty subsets (=blocks)?

\noindent Example: distributing students among Zoom breakout rooms.

\noindent \textbf{Solution}

\noindent There are 4 cases, depending on the number of blocks:
\begin{itemize}
    \item 1 block: $abcd$
    \item 2 blocks: $abc|d \quad abd|c \quad acd|b \quad bcd|a \quad ab|cd \quad ac|bd \quad ad|bc$
    \item 3 blocks: $ab|c|d \quad ac|b|d \quad ad|b|c \quad bc|a|d \quad bd|a|c \quad cd|a|b$
    \item 4 blocks: $a|b|c|d$
\end{itemize}
\noindent Total: 1+7+6+1=15.

\noindent Exercise: Solve the analogous problem for a 5-element set.

\textbf{Permutations of subsets} \\
\hline
\end{tabular}

\vspace{0.2cm}

\noindent
\begin{tabular}{|p{0.95\textwidth}|}
\hline
\textbf{Problem} \\
\hline
Given 34 people, how many ways are there for \textit{at most} 4 of them to wait in line? \\
\hline
\end{tabular}

\vspace{0.2cm}

\noindent
\begin{tabular}{|p{0.95\textwidth}|}
\hline
\textbf{Solution} \\
\hline
Let us divide the set of possibilities into cases:
\begin{itemize}
    \item The line has 0 people. There is 1 way this can happen.
    \item The line has 1 person. There are 34 ways this can happen.
    \item The line has 2 people. There are $34 \cdot 33 = 1122$ ways.
    \item The line has 3 people. There are $34 \cdot 33 \cdot 32 = 35904$ ways.
    \item The line has 4 people. There are $34 \cdot 33 \cdot 32 \cdot 31 = 1113024$ ways.
\end{itemize}
Hence the total number of ways is
\[ 1 + 34 + 1122 + 35904 + 1113024 = \textbf{1150085}.\]
Note that we used both Addition and Multiplication Principles. \\
\hline
\end{tabular}

\section*{The Division Principle}

The Division Principle is used in situations where each object of interest is counted several times (a fixed number).

\noindent\fbox{\parbox{\dimexpr\linewidth-2\fboxsep-2\fboxrule\relax}{
\textbf{Problem}

How many diagonals are there in a convex $n$-gon?
}}

\begin{center}
\begin{tikzpicture}
    % Placeholder for the first diagram
    \node at (0,0) {[Diagram: Octagon with all diagonals]};
\end{tikzpicture}
\qquad
\begin{tikzpicture}
    % Placeholder for the second diagram

\end{tikzpicture}
\end{center}

\noindent\fbox{\parbox{\dimexpr\linewidth-2\fboxsep-2\fboxrule\relax}{
\textbf{Solution}

For each of the $n$ vertices, there are $n-3$ diagonals connecting to it. Each diagonal is counted twice. \textbf{Answer:} $n(n-3)/2$.
}}

\textbf{The Subtraction Principle}

\vspace{0.2in}

\noindent
The Subtraction Principle is used in situations where it is more practical to count the objects of interest together with some other objects, and then subtract the number of those extra objects.

\vspace{0.2in}

\noindent
\textbf{Problem}

\noindent
How many ways are there to place 5 people named Amy, Bernie, Cory, Donald, and Elizabeth on the stage so that Amy and Bernie are \underline{not} adjacent to each other?

\vspace{0.2in}

\noindent
\textbf{Solution}

\noindent
$5! - 4! \cdot 2 = 72$.

\section*{}
Subsets and binary strings

\section*{}
\noindent\hrulefill
\textbf{Theorem}
\noindent\hrulefill
\newline
A $k$-element set has $2^k$ subsets.

\begin{equation*}
    \boxed{k=3} \quad \emptyset \quad \{A\} \quad \{B\} \quad \{C\} \quad \{A,B\} \quad \{A,C\} \quad \{B,C\} \quad \{A,B,C\}
\end{equation*}

\section*{}
\noindent\hrulefill
\textbf{Proof}
\noindent\hrulefill
\newline
The subsets of a $k$-element set are encoded by binary strings of length $k$, i.e., by $k$-letter words in a 2-letter alphabet:

\begin{equation*}
\begin{array}{cccccccc}
    \emptyset & \{A\} & \{B\} & \{C\} & \{A,B\} & \{A,C\} & \{B,C\} & \{A,B,C\} \\
    000 & 100 & 010 & 001 & 110 & 101 & 011 & 111
\end{array}
\end{equation*}

The number of such words is $2^k$.

\noindent Boolean functions

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\fbox{\begin{minipage}{\dimexpr\linewidth-2\fboxsep-2\fboxrule\relax}
\textbf{Problem}

\vspace{0.2em}
How many Boolean functions in $k$ variables are there?
\end{minipage}}

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\fbox{\begin{minipage}{\dimexpr\linewidth-2\fboxsep-2\fboxrule\relax}
\textbf{Solution}

\vspace{0.2em}
Each of the $k$ input bits can take value 0 or 1. There are $2^k$ possible input binary strings. A Boolean function can send each of them to either 0 or 1. So the total number of possibilities is $2^{2^k}$.
\end{minipage}}

\vspace{0.5em}
\noindent\fbox{\begin{minipage}{\dimexpr\linewidth-2\fboxsep-2\fboxrule\relax}
\textbf{Exercise}

\vspace{0.2em}
Count microchips with $k$ binary inputs and $\ell$ binary outputs.

Two microchips are considered identical if they compute the same function \{binary strings of length $k$\} $\rightarrow$ \{binary strings of length $\ell$\}.

For $\ell = 1$, you should recover the previous answer.
\end{minipage}}
